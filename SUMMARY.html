Predicting League of Legends Match Outcomes from the First 15 Minutes

Can a computer guess who is going to win a professional video-game match just by looking at the first 15 minutes? That was the question we set out to answer — and the short version is: usually yes, but the close games stay genuinely hard.

What we looked at

League of Legends is a team game where two squads of 5 players try to destroy each other's base. A match usually lasts around half an hour. We wanted to know how much of the eventual outcome is already visible from what happens in just the first 15 minutes — the early gold collected, who took the first tower, who killed the first monster, and so on. To do that, we used a public archive containing every professional match played in 2025, which gave us roughly 10,000 games from 45 different leagues. For each game we wrote down about 46 small facts about its early stage and asked the computer to learn the connection between those facts and the final result.

How we tried to answer it

There is no single best way to teach a computer to make predictions, so we tried 5 different methods side by side. Some are simple and easy to explain, like a decision tree that asks a chain of yes-or-no questions. Others are more elaborate, like a "random forest" that combines hundreds of small trees and lets them vote on the answer. Each method has its own strengths and blind spots, and comparing them on the exact same data is the fairest way to see which kind of thinking actually works for this problem.

We also asked a second question: out of all those 46 clues we gave the computer, which ones really matter? Some turned out to be repeating the same information in slightly different forms, and others added almost nothing. 3 independent techniques were used to trim the list down to the truly useful clues, and we kept only the ones that all 3 techniques agreed on.

What we found

The early game does forecast the late game, but it does not lock it in. Our best models were correct about 3 out of every 4 times (around 75%). That sounds impressive, but a much simpler rule — "whichever team has more gold at the 15-minute mark wins" — is already correct nearly 73% of the time on its own. So the machine learning we did adds a real but modest improvement on top of common sense.

A more interesting picture appears when we split the games by how lopsided they are. When one team is already very far ahead at minute 15, almost any rule will guess the winner correctly — the game is essentially over. When the two teams are roughly even, even the best models hover near a coin flip. The real value of the prediction work shows up in the middle band: the close games where the lead is small but real. That is also, not coincidentally, where the games are most exciting to watch.

We also tested whether one role on the team matters more than the others. Players often say that the bot lane — the position responsible for sustained damage in the late game — is the most important. Our data agreed, but only slightly. Bot-lane leads at 15 minutes were the most strongly connected to victory of any single role, yet the gap between bot, jungle and mid was much smaller than the popular intuition would suggest. It is a team game, and the data is firm on that.

The clues that survived our trimming were the obvious economic ones: which team had more gold, more experience, and more minions killed at the 15-minute mark, plus which team took the first tower. Things like first blood, while dramatic, were less informative than they look — they usually translate into a small gold lead, and once you already know about the gold lead, the kill itself adds little. We also confirmed a long-running rumour about the game: playing on the blue side of the map is genuinely a small advantage, with blue winning about 53% of professional matches.

What did not work

Plenty of things we tried did not pan out, and that is worth reporting too. Adding 20 extra detailed measurements about each individual player's kills and deaths sounded promising on paper but barely moved the accuracy and was abandoned. Forcing more clues into the simpler models often made them worse, not better, because several of the clues were just rephrasings of each other. Published academic work on this same problem tops out around 77–78% accuracy, so our 75–76% is within a couple of points of what anyone has ever achieved. The remaining gap is not bad modelling — it is the genuine drama of the game itself.

What we built

Alongside the written analysis, we built a small interactive web app where anyone can explore the dataset, switch between the different prediction methods we tried, and type in a hypothetical fifteen-minute snapshot of a game to see what the model would predict. Everything is reproducible: the same data, run through the same steps, produces the same results.

The takeaway in one line

A professional League of Legends match is not decided at minute 15, but it is heavily foreshadowed. About 3 in 4 games can be called from the early minutes alone — and the remaining quarter is where the actual sport lives.
